Circuits in Series

A series circuit is a type of electrical circuit where components are connected one after another in a single path. This means that the current flows through each component in turn.



Key Features

  • Single Path : There’s only one path for the current to flow.
  • Same Current : The same amount of current flows through all components.
  • Voltage Division : The total voltage from the power source is divided among the components.

Examples

Simple circuit

Scenario: A 9-volt battery connected in series to three resistors: R1 = 3 ohms, R2 = 3 ohms, R3 = 3 ohms.

Total Resistance (R):
R = R1 + R2 + R3 = 3 ohms + 3 ohms + 3 ohms = 9 ohms

Total Current (I):
I = V / R = 9 volts / 9 ohms = 1 amp

Voltage across each resistor:
V1 = I × R1 = 1 amp × 3 ohms = 3 volts
V2 = I × R2 = 1 amp × 3 ohms = 3 volts
V3 = I × R3 = 1 amp × 3 ohms = 3 volts

Series Circuit with Different Resistances

Scenario: A 10-volt battery connected in series to two resistors: R1 = 6 ohms and R2 = 3 ohms.

Total Resistance (R):
R = R1 + R2 = 6 ohms + 3 ohms = 9 ohms

Total Current (I):
I = V / R = 10 volts / 9 ohms ≈ 1.11 amps

Voltage across each resistor:
V1 = I × R1 = 1.11 amps × 6 ohms ≈ 6.67 volts
V2 = I × R2 = 1.11 amps × 3 ohms ≈ 3.33 volts
